Pooling System

In the early stages of development, our vision for 'The Legend of Tronco' involved creating a hack-and-slash roguelite, necessitating the establishment of a system for spawning enemies across the map. To accommodate a wide variety of enemy quantities, we implemented a pooling system—a task that fell under my responsibilities.

My decision to undertake this task was rooted in my previous experience with a similar system, driven by the ambition to identify areas for improvement. However, the journey wasn't as straightforward as I had hoped. Numerous revisions and extensive refactoring were required to optimize the system for the benefit of both my designers and the overall game. In the upcoming sections, I'll delve into these transformative changes and the iterative process that led to a more refined implementation.

PoolsCharacter.h, this script duty is to pool an actor from the character class.

In the PoolsCharacter.cpp file, the PoolAtBegin method searches for instances of PooledCharacter in the world. This class represents the enemy type slated for pooling. Subsequently, the method unleashes these pooled objects into the world using the SpawnPooledObject method.

PooledCharacter.cpp, the character returns back to the pool from its own base class script via the method Deactivate.

I've configured the pool size for both ranged and melee enemies to be two each. However, during gameplay, I've restricted the spawning of only one melee enemy, concealing the other with the remaining two ranged enemies.

General System Programming

I also took charge of a few smaller yet critical tasks, such as the implementation of the save system. To provide context, when the player ascends to the next level in our roguelite, a new map/level loads, leading to the destruction of all assets. To ensure the preservation of key features, we needed a robust save and load mechanism.

Additionally, I oversaw and assisted in implementing the logic for a narrative cinematic cutscene. While it functions solely as an intro, it adds depth to the overall gaming experience.

I opted to employ the Game Instance for loading and saving our crucial variables, and in the process, I gained valuable insights into the effective utilization of Interfaces. Employing a straightforward getter and setter methodology, I could efficiently store variables and arrays, streamlining the process of managing critical game data.

The player character, BP_HeroCharacter_Base, serves as our controllable character. During the Begin Play event, the player stores information in Skills and Active Passives. Notably, there's a custom event named 'Lose Everything' that triggers exclusively when the player dies. During this event, all Active Passives are cleared, while Skills remain intact.

Throughout this game project, I've developed a deep appreciation for utilizing Interfaces. Not only does it contribute to saving memory and enhancing performance compared to Cast To nodes, but it also streamlines the processing, making it more comprehensible for the engine. I've personally implemented several interfaces, and I've encouraged my team to embrace this approach, resulting in a reduction of Cast To nodes in favor of interfaces. It's rewarding to see that the team has adopted this practice, evident in the increased utilization of interfaces in our project.

One of the intricate yet intuitively designed systems involves the player choosing a passive ability after completing a wave. The process is initiated through a custom event called 'Randomize Abilities,' which unfolds within a blueprint widget, allowing players to select their desired passive ability.

The 'Pick a passive ability' event triggers when the player successfully defeats all enemies in a wave, part of multiple waves. Notably, these acquired passive abilities are not saved in the event of the player's death

Randomize abilities is this large with even more to come inside functions.

Within the 'Randomize Three Passives' function, a straightforward process unfolds: it extracts elements from a data table named 'PassiveTable', then continues to randomize until three unique ones are obtained, as illustrated in the previous slide.

Returning to the Event Graph, the system has successfully assigned three unique passive names from the data table, fulfilling our requirements. Upon the player's selection of one passive, the game resumes with the 'Resume Game' function. The 'Set Name' interface is then employed to relay this information back to the player blueprint.

With the correct Row name from the 'Passive Table' data table, we incorporate the name into the UI using 'Add Ability List To UI.' This process is followed by implementing the logic behind the buffs with 'Add Passive By Name.' Finally, the game resumes with 'Start Wave' once again.

My team expressed the need for gamepad support, marking one of my most challenging and daunting experiences to date. It provided insights into the complexities of maintaining compatibility with both keyboard and gamepad inputs.

To address this, I leveraged Epic Games' own plugin, the 'Common UI Plugin,' for gamepad support. While it offered a straightforward solution, achieving a balance with keyboard support posed challenges. The intricacy arose when a control scheme worked seamlessly, but the other ceased to function entirely.

After experimenting with various ideas, I successfully implemented a solution using two boolean variables: 'Is Using Gamepad' and 'Is Using Keyboard.' These variables dynamically change based on whether the player activates the gamepad or keyboard, ensuring a seamless transition between control schemes."
